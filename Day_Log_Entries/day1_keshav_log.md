Spectral bands (your “brain waves”):
Mental fatigue reliably causes large increases in theta and alpha power, especially over central and frontal regions. 
Across 24 workload studies, frontal theta is the best single EEG index of cognitive workload; alpha decreases and beta increases also track load but less cleanly. 
In multitasking, theta and alpha power increase with subtask load up to a point, then “saturate” when performance collapses, so you can use these bands to detect both high load and overload/fatigue. 

ERP marker: P300
After long-term cognitive tasking, P300 amplitude in parietal areas is reduced under fatigue at high workload, showing fewer allocatable cognitive resources, while latency stays similar.
In sustained visual attention, mental fatigue makes P3 smaller and slower, paralleling slower reaction time and lower accuracy.

What is P300?
P300 is a positive EEG wave around 300 ms that appears when you notice and evaluate something important, and its size and timing give a window into your attention and information‑processing speed. 
These waves are important while noting the state of the person (tired/happy/energetic etc)

Advantages:
High temporal resolution: Millisecond‑level timing, ideal for tracking rapid workload changes and real‑time feedback.
Non‑invasive and portable: Frequently used in HCI, emotion, and workload recognition; suitable for real‑world monitoring.
Direct link to neural activity: Reflects the source of cognitive fatigue more directly than many peripheral measures.
Proven success in cognitive load/fatigue classification: Many ML/DL models reach >90% accuracy on workload/fatigue datasets.

Disadvantages:
Noisy, artifact‑prone signals: EEG is often “too noisy”; muscle, eye, and motion artifacts are common.
Setup and usability: Commercial systems can be hard to set up, especially for untrained users or patients.
Long‑term stability: Maintaining stable signal quality over long periods with dry/semi‑dry electrodes is difficult.
Movement and environment sensitivity: Real‑world, mobile, or dynamic use cases suffer from movement interference and inconsistent signals.
User comfort vs. signal quality trade‑off: More electrodes and tighter caps improve data but reduce comfort; dry electrodes improve comfort but must balance impedance and motion artifacts.

Young vs. older adults (N‑back/Sternberg):
In young adults, higher N‑back load produces reduced parietal P300 amplitude; older adults show generally smaller P300s and slower, less accurate performance.
With increasing set size in a Sternberg WM task, parietal P300 decreases in both ages, but frontal P300 increases in young adults only, consistent with flexible frontal recruitment; older adults show less frontal up‑modulation.
Broad lifespan data show P300 amplitude decreases and latency increases with age, especially after ~20 years.
