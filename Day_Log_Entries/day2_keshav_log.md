EEG (Electroencephalography) is a technique used to record the electrical activity of the brain through electrodes placed on the scalp. The brain consists of billions of neurons that communicate using electrical signals, and EEG captures these signals as waveforms over time. Different patterns in these brain waves are associated with different mental states such as alertness, relaxation, focus, drowsiness, and fatigue. Since EEG provides a direct measurement of brain activity, it is widely used in applications involving cognitive state analysis, emotion recognition, and fatigue detection.
Raw EEG signals are usually noisy and contain disturbances that do not originate from the brain. These disturbances, known as artifacts, can be caused by eye blinks, eye movements, muscle activity, head movements, or electrical interference from the surrounding environment. Because of this, raw EEG data cannot be directly used for analysis or machine learning. Preprocessing is required to clean the signals and improve their quality so that the recorded data reflects actual brain activity as accurately as possible.
MNE (MNE-Python) is one of the most widely used open-source libraries for EEG and other neurophysiological signal processing. It provides tools for loading EEG datasets, visualizing brain signals, filtering noise, detecting artifacts, and preparing data for further analysis. MNE simplifies many preprocessing tasks and helps researchers work with EEG data in a structured and efficient manner. It also provides visualization tools that make it easier to understand how brain signals change over time and across different channels.
The preprocessing pipeline typically involves loading the EEG recordings, inspecting the signals, applying filters to remove unwanted frequencies, reducing noise and artifacts, and segmenting the data into meaningful time windows. After preprocessing, the cleaned EEG signals are converted into features that can be used by machine learning models. This transformation allows complex brain activity to be represented in a form that algorithms can understand and use for tasks such as cognitive fatigue detection and vigilance estimation.
